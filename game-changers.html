01 · The time audit

What gets measured gets managed.

This is a simple sheet where you log your day in 30-minute blocks: what you did, the category it falls under, a focus rating from 1 to 5, and a quick note. It sounds tedious. It was the single thing that changed how I studied.

The point isn't to feel guilty about your time. It's to replace your guess about where your hours go with the actual data, so you can make one or two changes that compound over a whole prep cycle.

Insight 01

The truth about your time

At the end of a day or week, paste your log into an AI assistant and ask it to break down where your hours actually went. The gap between where I thought my time was going and where it really went was a wake-up call.

Insight 02

Your real peak hours

The focus column quietly reveals when your mind is genuinely sharp, not when you assume it is. Once you can see it, you can protect those hours for the hardest material instead of burning them on admin.

Insight 03

A test-day edge

If your sharpest hours don't line up with your DAT appointment time, shift your sleep and wake schedule in the weeks beforehand so you peak exactly when it counts. Most people never think to train their circadian rhythm for the test.

02 · Performing under pressure

When you know you can, but on the day you can't.

I've always been able to perform in practice and then freeze when it counts. On my first DAT, I knew the material cold, but the moment the test started my mind locked up. Thoughts stopped flowing. I was stuck in my own head.

I knew that feeling well. It used to follow me onto the soccer field, where I carried a heavy case of imposter syndrome: I'd started years after everyone else, nobody believed I belonged, and eventually I stopped believing it myself.

I didn't get the score I knew I was capable of that first time. I couldn't face going home, so I walked around downtown Vancouver for hours, full of shame and anger that the same feeling had chased me from the pitch all the way to the testing centre. That night I hit a wall, and out of it came a quiet vow: I would find an answer, whatever it took.

A few days later I stumbled onto a book called Letting Go. The idea is simple, and it undid years of self-sabotage.

Instead of fighting or burying an emotion, you let yourself fully feel it, and then you release it. I used it every single day while I studied for my second attempt, and studying stopped feeling like a fight. There was no resistance to push through. The verified scores on the main page are what came out the other side.

One exercise I leaned on hardest: I made a mock score report showing a perfect result, set it as my desktop wallpaper, and each day I'd look at it and ask myself, honestly, whether I believed I could walk in and earn it. Whatever doubt surfaced, I let go of. I wasn't perfect at it. There was still some doubt on my last day. But even done halfway, it worked.

One idea kept me going through the hard days: there is a finite amount of any feeling. Once you have genuinely let one go, it is gone for good. And if it resurfaces, that is not failure, it just means there was more of it left in the tank to release.

The releasing technique, in short

  1. Notice the feeling, and let it be there.
  2. Welcome it fully instead of resisting it.
  3. Ask yourself: could I let this go? Would I? When?
  4. Let it move through, and repeat as it resurfaces.
